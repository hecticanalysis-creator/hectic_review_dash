Classification is based on duties, not job title. If an employee routinely performs L2 work, they are entitled to L2 pay regardless of what the roster or contract says. Misclassification is the most common underpayment cause in the fast food sector.
FFIA Sunday rates differ by classification level. Permanent L1 employees receive 125% on Sunday. Permanent L2 and L3 employees receive 150%. Applying a flat Sunday rate across a mixed-level roster is a near-certain underpayment. This is unique to FFIA and frequently miscalculated.

No separate public holiday minimum under FFIA. The FFIA award text (clause 27.3) does not specify a 4-hour minimum for public holiday shifts — that rule exists in the Hospitality and Restaurant awards, not FFIA. For casuals, the standard 3-hour minimum engagement applies. Permanent employees are paid for actual hours worked at the public holiday rate. Always confirm with your payroll provider or IR advisor.

Top compliance risks — FFIA MA000003
✕
Sunday rates vary by level — L1 ≠ L2/L3 Permanent L1 gets 125% on Sunday. Permanent L2 and L3 get 150%. Applying a flat Sunday rate across a mixed-level roster is a near-certain underpayment. This is unique to FFIA and one of the most frequently miscalculated penalties in QSR.
✕
3-hour minimum for every casual engagement Casual employees must be paid for at least 3 consecutive hours each time they come in — per engagement, not per week. If a casual is called in to cover and you send them home after 90 minutes, you still owe 3 hours. Cutting a shift short doesn't change the minimum.
!
Annual rate update — 1 July every year Award rates increase each July after the Fair Work annual wage review. Missing the update date means every employee is technically underpaid from day one of the new financial year. Set a payroll calendar reminder for late June — don't assume your provider catches it automatically.
!
Casual conversion — employee choice pathway (2024 law change) Since the Closing Loopholes No.2 Act 2024, eligible casuals (6 months regular work; 12 months for small businesses) can request conversion to permanent employment in writing. You must respond in writing within 21 days. Not having a documented process for receiving and responding to these requests is a Fair Work audit trigger.
✕
Missed breaks create overtime liability, not just a breach If a shift exceeds 5 hours and an employee doesn't get their meal break, overtime rates apply from the moment the break should have been taken — until they receive it. Busy weekend rushes are the most common trigger. This is a pay obligation, not just a conditions matter.
!
10-hour break after overtime is mandatory An employee who works overtime must have 10 consecutive hours off before their next shift. If they return early (e.g. covering a sick call the next morning), double time applies until the 10-hour rest period is reached.
!
Annual leave loading — 17.5% or weekend penalty, whichever is higher Permanent employees taking annual leave receive 17.5% loading — or the penalty rate they would have earned on those days if that is higher. Staff who regularly work Saturdays or Sundays are often paid incorrectly at just 17.5% when their weekend penalty would have been higher.
✓
Classification must reflect actual duties — not job title You cannot keep someone on L1 rates because their title or contract hasn't been updated. Classification is determined by the skills and duties the employer requires — not the label on the roster. If someone is routinely doing L2 work, they are entitled to L2 pay from when they started doing it.
✓
Records must be kept for 7 years Employee classification, pay rates, start and finish times, overtime, allowances, and leave records. Fair Work requests these records first in an audit. The retention requirement is 7 years — not 5.
The FFIA is a national award — pay rates and conditions are consistent across all states. The state-specific impact is the Victorian public holiday calendar, which determines when the 225%/250% public holiday penalty rates apply.

⚠ AFL Grand Final Friday

The date is announced by the AFL each year — typically late September or early October. It is a Victorian public holiday and public holiday pay rates apply to all staff who work that day. Add it to your payroll calendar immediately once announced — it's easy to miss in the rush of September rosters.

⚠ Melbourne Cup Day

Applies within the Melbourne metropolitan area only. If any of your outlets are in regional Victoria, Cup Day is not a public holiday for those sites and ordinary rates apply. Confirm the applicable area for each outlet individually — don't assume a blanket Vic-wide treatment.

Substitute public holidays

When Christmas Day or Boxing Day falls on a weekend, a substitute weekday public holiday is observed. If an employee works both the actual day and the substitute day, the public holiday rate applies to only one of those days — the employee chooses which.

Reasonable request to work

Under the FFIA, you may request an employee work on a public holiday if the request is reasonable given the nature of the business. Employees may refuse on reasonable grounds. All requests and responses should be documented in writing.

Always confirm dates each year. The AFL Grand Final Friday date in particular must be added manually to your payroll calendar once the AFL announces it — typically August or September. Check business.vic.gov.au for the official Victorian public holiday list each year.
